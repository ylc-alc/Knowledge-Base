# Knowledge Summary
This guide provides a detailed breakdown of how to transition from descriptive writing to analytical academic writing, a common hurdle for dissertation students. It focuses on constructing arguments, maintaining a critical voice, and justifying theoretical choices. [cite: 10]

# Extracted Details
- **The Descriptive Trap**: Many students fail to achieve high marks because they summarize what they have read rather than analyzing its significance or validity. [cite: 10]
- **Argument Construction (Issue: Too few arguments)**:
  - Weak Approach: Using descriptive statements like "This paper will discuss..." [cite: 10]
  - Strong Approach: Using argumentative structures such as "This essay argues that [X] because of [A] and [B]." [cite: 10]
- **Critical Analysis (Issue: Too descriptive)**:
  - Definition: Critical analysis involves evaluating evidence, identifying gaps in existing research, and questioning the assumptions of other authors. [cite: 10]
  - Application: Instead of listing authors' points, contrast them: "While X argues for Y, this study observes Z, suggesting a limitation in X's framework." [cite: 10]
- **Justifying Frameworks (Issue: Unclear theoretical choice)**:
  - Requirement: It must be "obvious" why a specific theoretical framework is being used. [cite: 10]
  - Action: Explicitly state how the chosen theory provides the necessary tools to answer your specific research question. [cite: 10]
- **Developing a 'Critical Voice'**:
  - This involves moving away from being a "bystander" who reports facts to being an "author" who evaluates them. [cite: 10]
  - Higher grades (Distinction/High Pass) are typically reserved for work that demonstrates this voice. [cite: 10]

# Key Takeaways
- Analysis is the process of breaking down information to understand its internal logic and external relevance. [cite: 10]
- Every piece of evidence cited should serve the central argument of the thesis. [cite: 10]

# Actionable Tips
- **The "So What?" Test**: After writing a descriptive paragraph, ask "So what?" and answer it in the next sentence to ensure analytical depth. [cite: 10]
- **Linguistic Shift**: Replace "Author X says..." with "Author X's evidence supports the idea that..." to focus on the argument rather than the person. [cite: 10]
- **Introduction Hook**: Ensure your introduction clearly states your "Argument" (the 'because' part) rather than just the "Topic." [cite: 10]
